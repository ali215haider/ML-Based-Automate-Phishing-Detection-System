Email Phishing Detection

Safe Email Indicators
  • Personal Greeting
    Uses your actual name, not generic terms
  • Legitimate Sender
    From a verified, expected email address
  • Professional Language
    Proper grammar, spelling, and formatting
  • Expected Communication
    Related to services you actually use
Phishing Red Flags
  • Urgent Action Required
    "Act now," "expires today," "immediate action"
  • Generic Greetings
    "Dear Customer," "Dear User," "Hello there"
  • Suspicious Attachments
    Unexpected .exe, .zip, or document files
  • Requests for Sensitive Info
    Passwords, SSN, credit card details

URL Security Guidelines

Safe URL Characteristics
  • HTTPS Protocol - Look for the lock icon
  • Correct Domain - Matches the expected website
  • Proper Spelling - No typos in the domain name
  • Reasonable Length - Not excessively long
Suspicious Indicators
  • URL Shorteners - bit.ly, tinyurl.com, etc.
  • Too Many Subdomains - Multiple dots in domain
  • Suspicious Characters - @ symbols, hyphens
  • New Domains - Recently registered
Danger Signs
  • IP Addresses - Numbers instead of domain names
  • No HTTPS - Only HTTP, especially for login pages
  • Typosquatting - Similar to popular sites
  • Suspicious TLDs - .tk, .ml, .ga domains

Certificate Security Guidelines

Trusted Certificate Signs
  • Green Padlock - Secure connection indicator
  • Valid CA - Issued by trusted authority
  • Current Date - Not expired or future-dated
  • Domain Match - Certificate matches website URL
Certificate Warnings
  • Browser Warnings - "Not secure" messages
  • Expiring Soon - Certificate near expiration
  • Unknown CA - Unfamiliar certificate authority
  • Mixed Content - HTTP resources on HTTPS page
Certificate Red Flags
  • Expired Certificate - Past validity date
  • Self-Signed - Not verified by trusted CA
  • Domain Mismatch - Certificate for different site
  • Revoked Certificate - Blacklisted by CA

Security Best Practices

Prevention Strategies
Use Strong Passwords

Create unique, complex passwords for each account and use a password manager.

Enable Two-Factor Authentication

Add an extra layer of security with 2FA whenever possible.

Regularly update your browser, OS, and security software.

Verification Steps

When in doubt, contact the organization through official channels.

SSL Certificate Security

Valid Certificate Indicators
  • Padlock Icon
    Secure padlock displayed in browser address bar
  • HTTPS Protocol
    URL starts with https:// not http://
  • Trusted Certificate Authority
    Issued by recognized CA like DigiCert, Let's Encrypt
  • Domain Match
    Certificate domain matches the website URL
Certificate Red Flags
  • Certificate Warnings
    Browser displays security warnings or errors
  • Expired Certificate
    Certificate has passed its expiration date
  • Self-Signed Certificate
    Certificate not issued by trusted authority
  • Domain Mismatch
    Certificate issued for different domain
How to Check Certificate Details

Click on the padlock icon in your browser's address bar to view certificate information including:

  • Certificate authority and validity period
  • Domain coverage and alternative names
  • Certificate chain and trust path
  • Encryption strength and algorithms used

Common Phishing Examples

Example Subject: "URGENT: Your account will be suspended"
Red Flags:
  • Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")
  • Urgent language and threats
  • Requests to click links to "verify" account
  • Poor grammar and spelling errors
  • Sender domain doesn't match the bank
What to do: Contact your bank directly through their official website or phone number.

Example: "Your computer has been infected! Call this number immediately!"
Red Flags:
  • Unsolicited calls or pop-ups about computer problems
  • Requests for remote access to your computer
  • Demands for immediate payment
  • Claims to be from Microsoft, Apple, or other tech companies
What to do: Hang up or close the pop-up. Legitimate tech companies don't make unsolicited calls.

Example: "Someone posted a video of you! Click here to see it"
Red Flags:
  • Messages about embarrassing content
  • Fake notifications about tagged photos or videos
  • Links to sites that look like social media login pages
  • Messages from friends' compromised accounts
What to do: Verify with your friend through another communication method before clicking any links.

Step-by-Step Guide: Verify SSL certificate authenticity and security
Certificate Verification Steps:
  1. Click the Padlock Icon: Located in the browser's address bar next to the URL
  2. View Certificate Details: Click "Certificate" or "Connection is secure" option
  3. Check Certificate Authority: Verify it's issued by a trusted CA (DigiCert, Let's Encrypt, etc.)
  4. Verify Domain Match: Ensure certificate domain matches the website URL exactly
  5. Check Validity Dates: Confirm certificate is not expired or future-dated
  6. Review Certificate Chain: Verify the complete trust path to root CA
What to Look For:
Good Signs:
  • Green padlock icon
  • HTTPS protocol
  • Trusted certificate authority
  • Current validity dates
  • Domain name matches exactly
Warning Signs:
  • Browser security warnings
  • Expired or future-dated certificates
  • Self-signed certificates
  • Domain name mismatches
  • Unknown certificate authorities
